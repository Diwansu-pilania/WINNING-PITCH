# Presentation Scaffolds for Winning Pitches

This document provides proven presentation structures and storytelling frameworks for hackathons, startup pitches, product demos, investor presentations, and other high-stakes communication scenarios.

---

# I. Core Pitch Deck Structure (Hackathon Focused)

This structure is optimized for hackathon presentations where judges must quickly understand the problem, solution, impact, and execution quality.

| Slide | Title                  | Key Content                                      | Purpose                            |
| ----- | ---------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------- |
| 1     | Cover                  | Project name, logo, tagline, team name           | Create a strong first impression   |
| 2     | Problem                | Clear problem statement and supporting evidence  | Establish urgency and relevance    |
| 3     | Solution               | Overview of your solution                        | Introduce the core idea            |
| 4     | Market Opportunity     | TAM, SAM, SOM, target audience                   | Demonstrate scale and opportunity  |
| 5     | Competition            | Existing alternatives and competitors            | Show market awareness              |
| 6     | Competitive Advantage  | Unique differentiators and strengths             | Explain why your solution wins     |
| 7     | Product Demo           | Screenshots, workflow, features, benefits        | Make the solution tangible         |
| 8     | Traction               | MVP status, users, growth, validation            | Prove execution capability         |
| 9     | Customers & Engagement | User personas, feedback, retention, partnerships | Show product-market alignment      |
| 10    | Business Model         | Revenue generation strategy                      | Demonstrate sustainability         |
| 11    | Financials             | Metrics, projections, assumptions                | Show business viability            |
| 12    | Funding Request        | Investment needed and current backing            | Explain funding requirements       |
| 13    | Use of Funds           | Planned allocation of resources                  | Build confidence in execution      |
| 14    | Team                   | Team members, skills, experience                 | Establish credibility              |
| 15    | Advisors               | Mentors and industry experts                     | Add external validation            |
| 16    | Closing Slide          | Thank you, contact details, call to action       | Leave a memorable final impression |

# Recommended Hackathon Version

For most hackathons, judges care less about fundraising and more about innovation, execution, and impact.

A streamlined version is often more effective:

1. Cover
2. Problem
3. Why It Matters
4. Solution
5. Live Demo
6. Technical Architecture
7. Innovation & Differentiation
8. Impact
9. Future Scope
10. Team
11. Closing

Target duration:

* 5-minute pitch → 8–10 slides
* 10-minute pitch → 10–15 slides

# II. Storytelling Frameworks

Presentation structure matters as much as content. The following frameworks help transform information into compelling narratives.

---

## 1. The Hero's Journey

### Best For

* Vision presentations
* Product launches
* Startup pitches
* Innovation showcases

### Structure

#### Ordinary World

Describe the current situation.

#### Call to Adventure

Introduce the challenge or opportunity.

#### Trials and Obstacles

Show the difficulties faced.

#### Solution

Present your product, strategy, or innovation.

#### New World

Demonstrate the transformed future.

### Example

**Current Reality**

Developers spend hours integrating APIs.

**Challenge**

Fragmented documentation and tooling.

**Solution**

An AI-powered integration assistant.

**Future**

Developers ship integrations in minutes instead of days.

---

## 2. Situation → Complication → Resolution (SCR)

### Best For

* Strategy presentations
* Business updates
* Problem-solving discussions

### Structure

#### Situation

Where are we now?

#### Complication

What problem or change has occurred?

#### Resolution

What should we do next?

### Example

**Situation**

Customer support volume is growing.

**Complication**

Support costs are increasing rapidly.

**Resolution**

Deploy AI-powered support automation.

---

## 3. ABT (And, But, Therefore)

### Best For

* Elevator pitches
* Executive summaries
* Concise presentations

### Structure

> We have X opportunity AND Y advantage, BUT Z challenge stands in our way, THEREFORE we must take this action.

### Example

> We have strong customer demand AND a validated prototype, BUT onboarding remains difficult, THEREFORE we built an AI onboarding assistant.

---

## 4. Freytag Pyramid

### Best For

* Change management
* Training sessions
* Educational presentations

### Structure

#### Exposition

Introduce context.

#### Rising Action

Build tension around the challenge.

#### Climax

Reveal the key insight or breakthrough.

#### Falling Action

Explain implementation.

#### Resolution

Present the outcome.

---

## 5. Duarte Sparkline™

### Best For

* Persuasive presentations
* Investor pitches
* Product launches

### Core Principle

Create tension by alternating between:

* What is
* What could be

### Example

**Current Reality**

Manual processes waste time.

**Future State**

Automation saves hours daily.

Repeat this contrast throughout the presentation.

# III. Audience-Specific Story Triggers

Different audiences care about different outcomes.

---

## C-Suite Executives

### Primary Trigger

Market opportunity or competitive threat.

### Example Opening

> Three competitors captured 15% market share in the last quarter.

### Emotional Driver

Competitive advantage.

---

## Middle Management

### Primary Trigger

Efficiency and resource optimization.

### Example Opening

> Your teams could save 20 hours per week using this workflow.

### Emotional Driver

Operational excellence.

---

## Technical Teams

### Primary Trigger

Complexity and innovation.

### Example Opening

> This problem has remained unsolved for over a decade.

### Emotional Driver

Intellectual curiosity.

---

## Front-Line Teams

### Primary Trigger

Customer impact.

### Example Opening

> Let me tell you about a customer who struggled to complete a simple task.

### Emotional Driver

Helping people succeed.

# IV. The Hackathon V2 Winning Playbook (2026)

Winning hackathons is often more about storytelling, clarity, and relevance than technical complexity alone.

---

## The Four-Step System

### Step 1: Choose the Emotion First

Decide what judges should feel.

Possible emotions:

* Recognition
* Awe
* Tension
* Humor

Design the story around that emotion.

---

### Step 2: Anchor to Current Trends

Connect your idea to relevant industry conversations.

Examples:

* AI Agents
* MCP
* Autonomous Workflows
* Developer Productivity
* Security Automation
* Multi-Modal AI

Judges immediately understand relevance.

---

### Step 3: Build the Base Layer Manually

Before using AI tools, define:

* Problem statement
* README
* Architecture diagram
* Data model

Clear thinking beats excessive prompting.

---

### Step 4: Create Fast Feedback Loops

Ship quickly.

Test continuously.

Improve aggressively.

Successful hackathon teams optimize learning speed rather than coding speed.

---

# Winning Emotions

## Recognition

The audience thinks:

> I've experienced that exact problem.

Creates instant connection.

---

## Awe

The audience thinks:

> I didn't know that was possible.

Creates excitement.

---

## Tension

The audience thinks:

> This changes everything.

Creates urgency and curiosity.

---

## Humor

The audience laughs.

Positive emotion improves memorability and voting behavior.

---

# The Base Layer Rule

Before writing code, answer these four questions:

1. What problem are we solving?
2. Who experiences the problem?
3. Why does it matter?
4. Why is our solution different?

If the team cannot explain the project in four concise sentences, the idea is not yet clear enough.

# Quick Hackathon Pitch Formula

A simple winning formula:

### Problem

"This problem affects millions."

↓

### Stakes

"It costs users time, money, or opportunity."

↓

### Solution

"We built a better approach."

↓

### Proof

"Here's the demo."

↓

### Impact

"Here's what changes."

↓

### Future

"Here's where we go next."

This structure consistently performs well in hackathons because it is easy for judges to follow, remember, and evaluate.
